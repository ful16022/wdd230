Friendswood now has about 40,000 residents over 21 square miles in two counties – northern Galveston County and southern Harris County. Clear Creek, providing direct water access to the Gulf of Mexico through Clear Lake and Galveston Bay, cuts between the two counties.

Friendswood’s attributes make it an attractive place to live, with highly-rated school districts and easy access to jobs, entertainment, cultural sites, and events in Galveston and Houston. There is also easy transportation access by road, rail, or air, with multiple nearby highways and airports.

There are few signs of the early Friendswood settlers, but residents, fortunately, preserved or re-created sites that harken back to the community’s founding. In 1971, residents created a replica of the Frank J. Brown home; the first house built in Friendswood in 1895. The home, which includes structural items from the original house, is home to the Frank J. Brown Heritage Museum.

The museum includes:
  • A first-floor area that represents early Quaker homes.
  • Second-floor space for museum displays about Friendswood’s first 50 years, including photos and artifacts.
  • A barn built with lumber more than 100 years old.


It’s also possible you might find a fig tree or two in the area, providing a connection to the days when thousands grew here in the early 1900s.

But with all that’s changed, some things remain the same. Jocelyn Baker, a longtime resident who was Friendswood’s official historian, had a great love for this town. The former business owner, school teacher and newspaper editor died on June 5, 2022. She was 93 years old.

About Friendswood

Founding and Early 1900s

The Quakers’ religious movement started in England in the 1600s. It soon spread across the world, with some Quakers coming to the United States and founding the Pennsylvania colony. Later, Quakers, also known as Friends, were involved in settling the western territories.

﻿In 1895, two Quakers, Frank Jacob Brown and Thomas Hadley Lewis, decided to start a settlement on 1,538 acres of prairie near four creeks — Clerk, Chigger, Coward’s, and Mary’s. They purchased the land and recorded the name of Friendswood on July 15, 1895, at the Galveston Courthouse.

More than a dozen families traveled south and west to settle in the farming community. Most of the new residents, up until 1915, were part of the Quaker colony.

In Friendswood’s early years, the settlers’ lives focused on church and school. The devastating, but not fatal, 1900 Galveston storm demolished the building that housed the church and school. Lumber from the trees knocked down by the storm was used to build a new two-story building called The Academy. The Academy housed all grade levels until 1914. It was also the church sanctuary until 1949, when it was relocated to the current church building.

The land was blessed with fig orchards, satsuma orange orchards, and rice fields. The farming and production of Magnolia figs was a multimillion-dollar business and attracted more residents from the Houston area and beyond during the 1920s. There were almost 18,000 acres of figs in the early 1920s, with two fig preserving plants in Friendswood.

During the Depression, families moved to the area to create a new life and began farming. Oil fields discovered in the late 1930s provided jobs for newcomers. Houston businesspeople also started investing in land in Friendswood in the 1940s, especially near the creeks.

Still, Friendswood remained a small farming community until World War II, with fewer than 500 residents and just a church, school, post office, and grocery store.
